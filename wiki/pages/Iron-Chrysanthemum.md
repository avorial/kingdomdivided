## [¶](#symbolism-origin) **Symbolism & Origin**

The chrysanthemum is an imperial flower — a symbol of eternity and rule. By twisting it into **iron**, the Ai Hoku (Shogun) co-opted the Emperor’s flower, marking his own claim to permanence.

Each petal represents an *agent bound in service* — fragile in appearance, unyielding in reality.

Rumors claim the first Iron Chrysanthemums were drawn from **orphans of noble houses crushed by rebellion**, given masks of steel petals to hide their identities forever.

---

## [¶](#structure) **Structure**

**The Stem** – The unseen commander, answerable only to the Shogun. No name, no face. Some whisper the Stem is not one person but a *role passed between shadows*.

**Petals** – Field agents; spies, interrogators, assassins. They carry iron chrysanthemum pins as tokens of authority, but never openly.

**Leaves** – Informants and watchers woven into daily life: innkeepers, money-changers, concubines, even priests. Leaves are disposable, petals are not.

---

## [¶](#methods) **Methods**

**Interrogation:** Suspects are bound in iron chains and forced to kneel before a brazier where iron chrysanthemums are heated until red-hot. Few ever leave such chambers unmarked.

**Execution:** Victims are left with a chrysanthemum blossom — real or forged in iron — placed over their heart.

**Surveillance:** They embed themselves in households as servants, often for years, before striking. Many nobles fear their own attendants more than enemy soldiers.

**Communication:** Messages are written in floral codes, where arrangements of petals signify orders. A single bloom left in a teacup can mean death.

---

## [¶](#appearance-tools) **Appearance & Tools**

Agents wear **lacquered masks shaped with chrysanthemum petals**, often ash-grey or deep crimson.

Daggers curved like petals, forged with iron ridges, are their weapon of choice.

Armor is disguised beneath silk — they are meant to walk unseen among nobles, not march with armies.

---

## [¶](#reputation) **Reputation**

*Among Nobles:* Whispers of “the Iron Flower” are enough to silence dissent. Many prefer exile to the chance of being visited.

*Among Soldiers:* Feared as much as revered — some soldiers believe being conscripted into their ranks is a punishment worse than death.

*Among Commonfolk:* A mix of awe and dread; the flower is both a curse and a prayer. Some peasants secretly pin chrysanthemums to their doors for protection, not realizing it may draw attention.
